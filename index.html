About

I am Hongrui ZHU, a PhD student in Industrial Economics at UCASS. My research goes beyond traditional econometrics, applying AI and economic theory to address complex business and regulatory challenges in the digital age.

The central question: when markets are governed by algorithms and large language models, what new patterns of competition, pricing, and consumer behavior emerge that classical theory never anticipated? Answering this demands both rigorous economic intuition and executable computational tools.

Speed Bumps, Not Choke Points: What Trade Data Reveals About US Semiconductor Export Controls
Semiconductor Global Value Chain · UN Comtrade, 2020–2024
What We See
Global semiconductor exports peaked at ~$65B/month in mid-2022, then collapsed ~30% in the immediate aftermath of the October 2022 US export controls. But the disruption was short-lived: within six months, flows had largely recovered, and by late 2024 total exports surpassed their pre-shock peak. The controls landed — and the market absorbed them.
What's Surprising
Three findings cut against the narrative of effective containment. First, China and Hong Kong SAR emerged as the top semiconductor exporters by 2025 (~$12B/month each), suggesting China's role in the GVC deepened on both the supply and demand side. Second, China remained the dominant destination in 2024 ($234B), larger than in 2020 ($176B) — imports not only recovered but grew. Third, the product mix shifted in a counterintuitive direction: memory chips' share fell from 35.9% to 31.4%, while Processors & Controllers — the higher-value, more strategically sensitive category — actually grew from 51.5% to 54.4%.
What It Means
The semiconductor GVC is more elastic than export control frameworks assume. Three adaptation mechanisms appear to be at work: re-routing through entrepôt hubs (Hong Kong SAR's role as intermediary), domestic production scaling on the supply side, and front-loading of imports ahead of anticipated restrictions. Meanwhile, Vietnam ($28B → $48B) and India ($4B → $13B) show early signs of becoming alternative nodes — but at a scale that barely registers against China's gravitational pull. The central policy implication is sobering: unilateral export controls create adjustment costs and temporary shocks, but they are unlikely to durably restructure a GVC of this depth and complexity without multilateral coordination.

Simulating Tacit Collusion Under Algorithmic Pricing
Algorithmic Collusion in Q-Learning Pricing Agents
Research Question
How do supra-competitive prices arise in markets dominated by reinforcement learning algorithms — without any explicit human coordination?
Methodology
We build a MARL-based oligopoly sandbox and demonstrate that under specific exploration rates and discount factors, pricing algorithms inevitably converge toward tacit collusion.
Policy Relevance
Provides antitrust regulators (FTC, SAMR) with a quantitative toolkit for detecting and auditing algorithmic collusion in digital markets.

Consumer Demand Estimation with Large Language Model Agents
Estimating Consumer Demand via LLM Agents
Research Question
Traditional discrete choice models fail to capture consumer preferences when faced with unstructured textual product information. Can LLMs close this gap?
Methodology
We construct a population of heterogeneous consumer agents powered by GPT-4, feeding product text descriptions to reconstruct demand curves in a zero-shot setting.
Applications
Provides e-commerce platforms and dynamic pricing systems with more realistic zero-shot market testing environments, reducing reliance on costly field experiments.

Market Agent Lab

Adjust the market parameters and watch Q-Learning pricing agents compete in real time. This is the core simulation from Paper 1 — running directly in your browser.

Parameters
Oligopolists (N) 2
Number of competing firms in the market
Learning Rate (α) 0.10
Speed at which agents update Q-values
Discount Factor (δ) 0.95
Weight agents place on future profits
Economic Intuition
At the start, agents undercut to capture market share, driving prices toward the Nash equilibrium (Bertrand competitive price). But when there are few firms and the discount factor is high, algorithms spontaneously learn tacit collusion — pushing prices toward the monopoly level. No explicit agreement. Just algorithms learning to punish defectors.

Policy implication: In highly concentrated digital markets, even without inter-firm communication, simple pricing algorithms can reach an unspoken cartel — posing a severe challenge to existing antitrust law.
